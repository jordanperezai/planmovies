# Logo Rangers — Session 01: wordmark vs symbol, and the O

> Date: 2026-05-28
> Decision: PlanMovies brand logo. D1 wordmark-only / D2 wordmark + custom O (screen vs ticket) / D3 standalone symbol + lockup.
> Run independently, neutral prompts, no shared context. Bierut (verifier) read the actual files.

## Verdict (chairman synthesis)

**D2 — wordmark with the O of MOVIES as a cinema SCREEN (negative space). 5–1 for screen over ticket. Confidence: high.**

The one dissent (Haviv) argued the ticket, because a notch is the only *truly* distinctive subtractive cut and "you plan, you get a ticket" matches the verb. The verifier overruled it on evidence, not taste: in the actual files, the ticket notches and perforation **collapse into mush below ~40px** — they fail the favicon and white-chip tests. The screen-O is the only treatment that stays recognizable and one-color at 16px. Haviv + Bierut share one caution worth keeping: "ticket" says *event/night-out*, "screen" says *app* — if the brand leans event, the ticket could return as a **large-format-only flourish**, but it cannot be the favicon.

Where all six agree: **D3 is out** (a standalone symbol you'd have to teach, splits the lockup, drags back toward the buried literal-cinema marks). **D1 alone is out** as the final answer (it's where "clean but boring" came from; a plain wordmark dies at 16px). The answer is the wordmark carrying the name + one load-bearing letter carrying the mark.

### The design spec they converged on (this is the build)
1. **The favicon is the master, not the afterthought.** Design the standalone screen-O glyph at 16px FIRST, then set the wordmark around it. (Haviv, Scher, Hische, Bierut.)
2. **Wide cinemascope counter, not square** — ~1.85:1 to 2.35:1. A square reads "button"; a letterbox reads "screen." (Draplin, Leader.)
3. **Float it slightly high / off-center.** Symmetry reads as software; the off-center screen reads as "a place," a screen seen from a seat. That asymmetry is the life. (Draplin.)
4. **Heavy O ring** so the screen reads as a slot punched clean through mass. Mass around a precise hole = contrast that survives small. If the display face's O is too thin, switch faces before switching ideas. (Draplin.)
5. **Pure one-color negative space. Do NOT fill or tint the screen.** Gold stays on the word; the counter is pure absence. Tinting it makes it an icon and kills the discovery. (Leader, Bierut.)
6. **System:** glyph = screen-O alone (favicon / nav / app tile / unfurl); full lockup = PLAN (cream) + M[screen-O]VIES (gold), Clash Display 700; on white, invert the word to near-black and keep the gold O.
7. **Cut these (verifier):** beam (breaks square bounds), lit/sweep (gradient/glow, violates one-color), O3 subtle (vanishes to a blob at 16px), both ticket variants (notches die at favicon).

## Full responses

### 1. Sagi Haviv — Mark & longevity  (DISSENT: ticket)
My pick: D2, ticket. A wordmark with one engineered letterform detail is the appropriate solution — the name does the explaining, so the mark should not (rules out D3; a standalone symbol you must teach is a luxury this product can't afford, and a symbol earns meaning through years of use it doesn't have). D1 is honest but inert; nothing to hang recognition on at 16px, where you'd only ever show a cropped letter. D2 splits it correctly. Ticket over screen: a screen counter inside an O reads as a plain rounded rectangle, indistinguishable from a button. The ticket's notch is the distinctive move — a notched counter is unmistakably a ticket and nothing else, survives reduction because it's a single subtractive cut not a depicted scene, and matches the verb (you plan, you get a ticket). Reject D3, and reject treating the O detail as decoration: a fussy multi-cut notch dies at favicon size and looks like a flaw. One move that matters: engineer the notched O to work as a one-color knockout at 16px on white AND near-black; the standalone O is the favicon. If the O can't stand alone cleanly, the system fails. Cut once, cut deep, test small first. Confidence: high.

### 2. Paula Scher — Wordmark as identity  (screen)
D2, screen. The word is already the brand — "PLANMOVIES" bold, cream PLAN against gold MOVIES, fills its space and carries the identity without a mascot. Type IS the logo. But there's a hard reduction problem (16px favicon, tile) and a pure wordmark dies there, so it needs one load-bearing detail that survives amputation: the O's counter becomes the screen — a sly cinema cue in the word at large size, and cropped down you ship that single O-as-screen as the glyph. One letterform doing double duty. Reject D3 (a costume bolted on a name that doesn't need one). Reject the ticket counter (notches clog at 16px; "ticket" says transaction, not the social act of going together; the screen says the room everyone looks at). Reject D1 alone (mute at small sizes, which is where the link lives). One move: design the O-screen as an isolated glyph FIRST, then set the word around it — reverse the order. Favicon is the constraint, not the afterthought. Confidence: high.

### 3. Jessica Hische — Custom-letterform craft  (screen, the O)
D2, screen, the O in MOVIES. The letter must read as itself first, idea second. An O is the most forgiving counter — already a contained negative space, so reshaping its interior into a slightly-wider-than-tall screen is a quiet move, not a costume. You still read MOVIES; the screen is the second read. A ticket-notch on a counter reads as a defect at small size (a nick, a broken curve) and fails the favicon test; a screen is a clean geometric subtraction that survives to 16px. Reject D3 (a group-planning app lands in chat as a wordmark; a standalone pictograph forces two assets and says "cinema," not "us planning together"; no brand equity yet to earn a lockup). Reject the ticket counter and any detail on the V; don't let the screen touch the O's outer ring — it must float inside. One move: cut the screen counter as a true rounded rectangle holding the SAME stroke contrast as the rest of the wordmark, and test at 16px FIRST. If it closes to a dot at favicon size, widen the counter and thin the ring. The favicon is the master, not the hero. Confidence: high.

### 4. Aaron Draplin — Weight, guts, life  (screen)
D2, screen. D1 is where "clean but boring" comes from — two colors of type is a setting, not a logo; don't go back. D3 puts a separate symbol next to the word, and at 16px one wins, never both; it also drags toward the buried literal cinema stuff. Dead end. D2 hides the idea INSIDE the word where it has to live anyway. The O is already a circle, a circle is already a frame — so stop drawing a logo and start drawing a hole you look through. Screen over ticket: the ticket is the transaction; the screen is the thing you're all looking at together (people pointing at one glowing rectangle in the dark). One catch, the whole job: a plain rounded-rect counter is still boring. Give the screen a 2.35:1 cinemascope ratio, not square, and float it slightly HIGH in the O, like a screen seen from a seat looking up. That off-center is the life — symmetry reads as software, asymmetry reads as a place. Reject the ticket notch (fussy/transactional) and any second color inside the O. The move: wide screen pushed up off-center, O's stroke heavy enough that the screen reads as a slot punched clean through. Mass around a precise hole. Confidence: high on D2-screen; medium on whether the display face's O is fat enough — if thin, switch faces before switching ideas.

### 5. Lindon Leader — Negative space / the hidden idea  (screen)
D2, screen — the O's counter reshaped into a horizontal rounded rectangle floated inside the ring, leaving a thin letterbox of dark above and below. The brief's truest word is "together," but a logo can't draw a group at favicon size — so hide the one object every member is picturing: the screen they'll sit in front of. It lives in the O's counter for free; reshape the hole into a 1.85:1 letterbox and the second meaning is just there. That's the FedEx test: you either never notice it or can't unsee it. Reject the ticket (a payment artifact, not a togetherness artifact; the notch reads as damage at 16px). Reject D3 (a second thing to design and defend; no symbol beats the letters you own). Reject D1 as final (surrenders the gift the name hands you). The one move: do NOT recolor the screen gold — keep the mark one-color, the screen is pure absence like any other counter. Test at 16px in black-on-white and white-on-black; if the letterbox survives both, ship; if it closes, widen the counter, don't add a stroke. Confidence: med-high.

### 6. Michael Bierut — System & application  (VERIFIER, read the files; screen)
Read the artifacts and live nav. What I saw: the wordmark is the strongest thing in the files; the live nav (`index.html:2338`) already runs PLAN(cream)+MOVIES(gold) Clash Display 700 at 21px and holds — the cream/gold split IS a system. O treatments at real sizes: **O-as-screen 16:9** survives at 16/20px and on the white chip (a gold tile with a clean horizontal slot) — the only O that reduces cleanly. O3 subtle collapses to a blob at 16px. O-as-ticket-glyph: the 8px end-notches + 2.5px perforation vanish into mush at 16–18px — reads as a lozenge. Ticket-shaped-hole: notch-pinch circles disappear below ~40px. Beam breaks the square bounding box (unusable favicon). Lit/sweep rely on opacity/gradient — violate one-color/no-glow. Pick: **D2, screen (the 16:9 `#o2` cut)** — only treatment that earns its keep at every size and stays one-color; ticket is warmer but only exists above 40px (decoration, not a mark). Cut: beam, lit, sweep, O3, both ticket variants. System: small mark = the screen-O alone (favicon/nav/tile/unfurl); full lockup = PLAN(cream)+M[screen-O]VIES(gold) Clash 700; on white invert word to near-black, keep gold O. Owned blindspot: picking the safe survivor risks sanding off warmth — "ticket" says event, "screen" says app; if the brand is the night out more than the software, revisit ticket as a large-format-only flourish, but it can't be the favicon. Confidence: high on screen-over-ticket and the cuts; medium on screen-vs-pure-wordmark.
