## What We Researched

The session started with Jordan wanting to understand the ethos before touching the rabbit hole section. Four research agents ran in parallel: the movie, the disclosure timeline, the cultural moment, the testimony.

What came back was more complete than expected.

John Williams came out of retirement at 93 to score this. His 30th Spielberg collaboration. He suggested four other composers first. Then agreed to do it himself. That detail alone tells you the weight Spielberg placed on this.

Koepp wrote 42 drafts. When asked about the internet theory that the government recruited Spielberg to soft-disclose through entertainment, he said: "I will say I was never contacted by a member of the government on this. Unless I was neuralized and don't remember."

The real-world timeline reads like a screenplay. Obama says "they're real" on a podcast (February 14). Goes viral. Trump orders full declassification five days later, calling it classified information. The Pentagon launches WAR.GOV/UFO. One billion visits. Grusch under oath to Congress, facing federal perjury charges, saying "we have recovered non-human biologics." Senate Majority Leader Schumer drafts legislation to seize "recovered technologies of unknown origin" from private contractors via eminent domain.

You don't write eminent domain provisions for things that don't exist.

The question Jordan came in with was "what's the ethos." The answer is: the ethos is that the topic stopped being embarrassing. 75% of Americans already believe other life exists in the universe. The shift wasn't people changing their minds. It was the institutions catching up.

## The Flat List vs. The Story

The original rabbit hole was a flat list of clips: Obama, Trump, Grusch, Fravor, Pentagon. Each one dropped in the same format with the same visual weight. It told you what to watch but not why it mattered.

The restructure came from sitting with the research. The four layers aren't arbitrary categories. They're a narrative sequence.

First you show the witnesses. Decorated military officers under oath. Anyone who might dismiss this as fringe stops at a Navy TOPGUN commander describing physics that "no technology we have or will have in 20 years" can explain. The 60 Minutes piece alone does what hours of argument can't.

Then the government's own evidence. Pentagon-released footage. An object that goes from stationary to warp speed instantaneously. Not a theory. The government's own files.

Then the political chain. Obama. Trump. The fact that a former and current president both engaged publicly shifts the floor of what's considered legitimate.

Then GO DEEPER, for the people who are now hooked and want the full story.

Each layer earns the next one. The person who taps out after 60 Minutes still got the core argument. The person who watches Rogan's two-hour Fravor interview went somewhere they didn't expect when they tapped a movie event link in the family WhatsApp.

## The Rangers Split

The page structure debate went 3-2. Frisby, Kim, and Shadeed said one page. Johnson and Murthy said two.

Murthy's dissent was the most interesting. Not "it's too long" but "it deserves its own URL." The rabbit hole as a separate shareable artifact that evolves as new files drop. The crew page changes as people RSVP. The movie page stays static after you watch the trailers. But the evidence page changes every time the Pentagon releases another batch.

That argument will come back. Jordan kept it one page for now because the transition beat IS the product. But the idea of a living, linkable evidence archive with its own URL has momentum.

## Five Personas Walk In

The family panel ran for the first time. Five people, five distinct reactions to the same flow.

The crew list and numbering worked for everyone. "You're #2 on the crew" gives Marco early-adopter energy, makes Carmen feel accomplished, gives Ray a receipt, gives Sofia the only hook she needed. That single mechanic is doing more conversion work than any copy on the page.

Lisa's take on the UAP headlines was the most useful feedback of the session. Not because the fix was to remove them. Jordan overruled that. But her framing was exact: "You're making the page do two jobs and one of them undermines the other." The RSVP page's job is conversion. The evidence is there to immerse, not to warn. She felt warned. That gap is worth watching.

Ray wanted the date bigger. "The date is the single most important piece of information on this page and you put it in the smallest text on the screen." He's right. Fixed.

Carmen thought RSVP meant ticket. The gap between "you're on the list" and "you still need to buy your way in" is invisible unless you name it. One sentence under the submit button closed it.

## What Shifted

The site was built for Jordan. Someone who follows UAP news, knows who Grusch is, watched the 60 Minutes piece when it aired. The research session confirmed: that's not the audience.

The audience is Aunt Lisa, who gives the page 4 seconds. Uncle Ray, who types his middle name in the name field. Carmen, who would call Jordan about "+1."

The research gave the ethos. The personas gave the filter. Together, they're the same thing: understand the person on the other end, and design for the version of them that just opened a link they weren't sure they wanted to tap.

The link still hasn't been sent.
