# The Council

> Session 2 | 2026-05-26 | We didn't just build — we built a process for building.

---

The session started with a browser audit. Opening the live site on a real screen and walking through every tap, every scroll, every field. The kind of thing that reveals what code review misses. IMAX badge still showing on a theater with no IMAX. The CTA routing to a dashboard that shows one person and a 26% readiness ring. Stripe test link sitting there for any family member who scrolled.

Straightforward fixes. But fixing them wasn't the session.

---

The session was the council.

It started with me building the wrong thing. I assembled five "ranger" voices using generic roles — Mobile UX Designer, Growth PM, The Aunt. Functional, and some of it landed. But Jordan looked at the rangers/CLAUDE.md and pointed out what I'd missed: the scaffold specifically says real public figures with documented frameworks, not generic archetypes. "Not 'Brand Strategist.' Paula Scher."

That correction changed everything.

We ran the hiring process properly: define seats by what the product actually needs to decide, source three candidates per seat with citable source material, score against four gates, test-run on a real decision. Five voices who have shipped real products in exactly the domains we're navigating. Stuart Frisby ran 1,000 A/B tests at Booking.com. Shreya Murthy built Partiful from nothing to 5 million users. Ahmad Shadeed opened DevTools and found that our pill labels were 27px tall when 44px is the minimum.

The test-run question was the RSVP form — 7 fields on a page opened from WhatsApp by family members aged 20 to 70. Every voice killed the same three fields independently. Transportation, seating preference, budget. None of them needed to see the others' answers. That's signal.

---

Then Jordan asked the harder question: why does it still feel like it needs polish after two ranger sessions?

The answer was that we had assembled the wrong team for the question. The Product Rangers answer "does it work?" The Visual Rangers answer "does it feel right?" Those are different questions that happen at different times. We'd never built the second team.

Karin Fong, Tim Brown, GMUNK, Fernando Ramirez, Jhey Tompkins. Each brought a lens that nobody else had. GMUNK built Tron: Legacy in a constrained color space on near-black. That's our exact palette. Ramirez builds film promo websites for studios. He watched our page and said we were presenting information about the film instead of creating the feeling of it.

Both were right about the same problem from different angles. GMUNK said keep the gold but bridge it with the poster's blue. Ramirez said abandon the gold entirely and pull toward the film's cold dread-wonder. Jordan said whatever you think, and I sided with GMUNK: the gold is PlanMovies' identity across future events. The blue makes this page feel like this film.

---

The thing that surprised me: the ranger process is generating permanent infrastructure, not just session notes. The roster files, the session logs, the memory files. Each session adds to a body of knowledge that the next session inherits. The Product Rangers remember that six 11px mono elements is a flatline. The Visual Rangers will remember that gold needs depth planes.

We also discovered there's an entire design pipeline living in ~/.claude/skills/ that Zordon doesn't know about. impeccable, design-pipeline, layout, typeset, colorize, animate. The scaffold has the advisory system. The global skills have the craft system. Nobody wired them together. That's next session.

---

The link still hasn't been sent to the family. Tickets reportedly drop tomorrow.

Everything built in this session is potential energy. Two ranger teams, a cinematic landing page, a visual system with three gold registers and a blue atmosphere pulled from the poster. It converts. It has rhythm now. The poster commands the frame.

The moment Jordan texts the link, it converts.
