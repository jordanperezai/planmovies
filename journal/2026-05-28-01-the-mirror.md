# The Mirror

> Session 11 | 2026-05-28 | Design quality + architecture exploration
> Key discovery: "The poster was always the page. We just finally got out of its way."

---

## The Comparison

The session started with a screenshot. Not a feature request. Not a bug. Jordan opened the Partiful screenshots and asked the simplest possible question: why do we look like a vibe coded app?

The honest answer was: we were one. A dark admin dashboard with gold accents. Forms stacked on top of each other. Everything the same density. Nothing breathing. The movie — the actual $200M Spielberg production whose poster we had access to — was hiding behind a text header that said "The Crew."

The redesign wasn't technically complex. It was one CSS block and a layout reorder. The hero became the poster. The poster became the page. The form moved into a card with 24px margins. And suddenly it looked like an event you'd want to attend.

---

## The Numbers

Seven commits in one session. The cleanup work that should have been spread across weeks got done in hours:

- Uppercase: 29 → 7. Section headers are sentence case now. "The Crew" instead of "THE CREW."
- Letter-spacing: 71 → 24. The spray removed.
- Font-size declarations: 252 now use tokens instead of raw pixels.
- Form labels: actually linked to their inputs.
- Images: lazy loaded.

Numbers are satisfying. But the thing Jordan said — "whoa! so much better" — wasn't about the numbers.

---

## The Architecture Question

The second half of the session was something different. Jordan asked whether the Movie tab should be a side panel. Three personas voted no. Then Jordan asked whether the current two-tab setup was even right. Three personas voted no again.

The insight wasn't that the side panel was bad. The insight was that the separation was the problem. "The Movie" and "The Crew" tell one story together. Separately, each is half an argument. The evidence page makes no sense without the film. The film page makes no sense without the evidence. The Reality Break — "The movie is based on real events. These events are happening now." — only works if both sides are on the same scroll.

So by the end of the session, we had a mockup (v4) and a direction, but not an implementation. The right architecture is clear. The merged page is next.

---

## The Mockup

The three-act structure landed right:

Act 1: The Movie. Not a press kit. A poster, a tagline, a cast row you can swipe. Two filmmaker quotes. A one-line pitch. Then the form.

Act 2: The Turn. A single moment, full-width. "The movie is based on real events. These events are happening now." The pivot from entertainment to evidence.

Act 3: The Evidence. The timeline. 1947 to 2026. Every president. Every general. Every intelligence director. The weight compounds as the decades get closer to now. The featured nodes break the vertical rail. The 2023 congressional hearing gets a video card. The 2026 file drops get a gold-bordered section with a link to war.gov.

And at the bottom, a toggle: Timeline or By Authority. The timeline tells the story. The authority view lets you scan all six presidents in a row, which hits differently.

---

## What Shifted

The page was always trying to be two things: an event invitation and an evidence archive. The tab structure meant it failed at both. The invitation was cluttered. The evidence was buried.

One scroll fixes both. The invitation is at the top. The evidence earns its place below. People who just want to RSVP are done at screen 3. People who want to understand why this movie exists have 80 years of evidence waiting below the fold.

The poster was always the page. We just finally got out of its way.
