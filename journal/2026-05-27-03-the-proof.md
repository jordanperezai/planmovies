## The Pivot

About halfway through, Jordan asked about the payment link. We'd been talking about it in theory for two sessions. He wanted to test the real thing — the platform model, not just the family version.

Two hours later we had proof.

Stripe Connect Express. A new Cloudflare Worker. Three new Supabase tables. A `POST /api/events/create` that hit Stripe's API on a connected account and returned a real Payment Link with a $2.50 platform fee baked in. Then Jordan opened the link, typed in the test card, and paid $20.50.

The confirmation page said "A payment to PLANMOVIES will appear on your statement."

That's the whole business.

## The Number

The math is clean. Crew pays $20.50. Stripe takes $0.89. Organizer gets $17.11 (covers their $15.46 Fandango ticket). PlanMovies gets $2.50.

At 1,000 events per month, 10 crew members per event, that's $25,000 per month. Pure platform revenue. No inventory. No support burden. No refund risk on physical goods. The organizer runs the event. PlanMovies takes $2.50 per seat.

Jordan asked: "is this profitable?" Yes. By design. The fee is invisible to the organizer. The crew member pays a clean number. Stripe handles the split automatically via `application_fee_amount`. No manual transfers, no invoice reconciliation, no chasing anyone.

The test payment worked on the first try after the RLS fix. The organizer just taps a button once to connect their bank. Everything else is automated.

The PLANMOVIES statement descriptor was the moment that made the platform feel real. Not "Jordan Perez" or "Arugami." PLANMOVIES. That's a brand. That's a company.
